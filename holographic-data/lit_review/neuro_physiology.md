# Long-Range Temporal Correlations, Fractals, and Criticality in Neuroscience & Physiology — A Method-Focused Review for ML Data-Theory

*Lit-review prepared 2026-06-14 to import measurement methods/concepts into the (β, γ) phase-diagram / edge-of-chaos data project. Web search **was available**; citations were verified against journal/PubMed/arXiv pages.*

## Overview

Neuroscience and physiology have a 30-year, methodologically mature tradition of asking exactly the question this project asks: *does a measured time series carry long-range dependence, fractal/scale-free structure, and is the generating system poised near a critical point ("edge of chaos")?* Three method families dominate and all transfer cleanly to token/representation sequences:

1. **Detrended Fluctuation Analysis (DFA)** and its multifractal extension (MFDFA) — robust estimation of a Hurst-like scaling exponent α from a single, possibly non-stationary, series. Directly maps onto this project's **Hurst axis** and is the gold standard for measuring **long-range temporal correlations (LRTC)**.
2. **Avalanche / crackling-noise scaling** — power-law size & duration distributions (exponents τ, α_dur), the size-vs-duration scaling exponent 1/σνz, the **branching parameter m≈1**, and the **crackling exponent-relation test**. Maps onto the project's **β (power-law decay)** and **criticality / edge-of-chaos hypothesis**, and supplies *internal-consistency* tests that go beyond fitting a single slope.
3. **Multiscale entropy (MSE)** — a complexity measure that peaks for structured long-range-correlated signals and is *low* for both white noise and over-regular signals. This is the closest neuroscience analogue to the project's **"Maximum Effective Complexity at the edge of chaos"** hypothesis.

A recurring, hard-won lesson from this literature is the **criticality caution**: a single power law is weak evidence; the field now demands *multiple independent signatures plus an exponent-relation that they jointly satisfy*, and explicitly controls for power-law-mimicking artifacts (subsampling, finite size, neutral/stochastic models). This caution is the most important import for any "edge-of-chaos" claim in the ML project.

## Long-range temporal correlations (DFA in EEG/MEG)

**Linkenkaer-Hansen et al. (2001)** is the seminal demonstration that ongoing brain oscillations are not memoryless. The *amplitude envelope* of band-pass-filtered alpha (~10 Hz) and beta (~20 Hz) oscillations, recorded with EEG/MEG, shows power-law autocorrelation over time scales of 5–300 s — correlations persisting over *thousands* of oscillation cycles. They quantified this with **DFA** (scaling exponent α ≈ 0.6–0.9, well above the 0.5 white-noise value) and cross-validated with 1/f power spectra and a power-law decay of the autocorrelation function. This established LRTC as a *signature of criticality* in the brain.

**DFA mechanics (Peng/Hardstone).** DFA: (i) integrate the (mean-subtracted) series into a "profile"/random walk; (ii) split into windows of size n; (iii) locally detrend (linear or polynomial) within each window; (iv) compute RMS residual fluctuation F(n); (v) fit F(n) ∝ n^α on log-log axes. α=0.5 ⇒ uncorrelated; 0.5<α<1 ⇒ persistent LRTC; α>1 ⇒ non-stationary/unbounded; α relates to the Hurst exponent (H≈α for fractional Gaussian noise) and to the power-spectral exponent β_PSD via β_PSD = 2α−1. DFA's value over plain spectral/Hurst estimation is robustness to polynomial trends and mild non-stationarity. **Hardstone et al. (2012)** is the canonical pedagogical tutorial with reference code (NBT toolbox).

## Neuronal avalanches & criticality

**Beggs & Plenz (2003)** discovered *neuronal avalanches*: spontaneous bursts of LFP activity in cortical slice cultures and acute slices whose spatiotemporal sizes follow a **power law with exponent τ ≈ 1.5** (size distribution P(s) ∝ s^{−1.5}, the mean-field directed-percolation value), separated by silent periods. They introduced the **branching parameter** m = (expected number of descendant electrodes activated by one ancestor); they measured **m ≈ 1**, the hallmark of a critical branching process (m<1 subcritical/dying, m>1 supercritical/exploding, m=1 critical/marginally sustained). Avalanche patterns were stable for hours and reproducible — arguing the critical state is an organizing principle, not noise.

**Friedman et al. (2012)** raised the bar from "a power law" to **full crackling-noise criticality** in high-resolution cortical-culture data. They reported a self-consistent exponent set — size τ ≈ 1.6, duration α_dur ≈ 1.7, and the size-given-duration exponent **1/σνz ≈ 1.3** — and, crucially, showed that the **average avalanche temporal profile collapses onto a single universal (roughly parabolic) scaling function**, plus sub- and super-critical samples bracketing the critical point. This "shape collapse" is far stronger evidence than any single slope.

Self-organized criticality (SOC) provides the conceptual backdrop: **Bak, Tang & Wiesenfeld (1987)** showed dissipative spatially-extended systems can self-tune to a critical point with 1/f noise and scale-free avalanches; the brain is the most-studied candidate SOC system (reviews: Chialvo 2010; Beggs & Timme 2012).

## 1/f noise & E/I balance

Cortical field potentials have a broadband **aperiodic 1/f-like power spectrum** P(f) ∝ 1/f^χ superimposed on oscillatory peaks. Two ideas connect this to criticality and to the data project:

- **E/I balance ↔ spectral slope.** **Gao, Peterson & Voytek (2017)** built a model (and validated it on rat LFP & macaque ECoG) showing the **aperiodic slope χ is a readout of the excitation/inhibition (E/I) ratio**: more excitation ⇒ *flatter* spectrum (smaller χ), more inhibition ⇒ *steeper* spectrum. This is a cheap, single-channel proxy for a control parameter that tunes a network toward/away from criticality. The spectral slope is the frequency-domain twin of DFA's α (β_PSD = 2α−1), so a *slope is itself a long-range-dependence exponent*.
- **Criticality emerges at balanced E/I.** Multiple models (e.g. Poil et al. 2012, "critical-state dynamics… emerge from balanced E/I") show avalanche criticality and oscillation LRTC co-emerge precisely when excitation and inhibition are balanced — i.e., E/I balance *is* the brain's tuning knob toward the edge of chaos. This is the biological analogue of finding a (β, γ) sub-region where data is maximally complex.

## Physiological fractals & multiscale entropy

**Peng et al. (1995)** applied DFA to inter-beat (RR) interval series of the human heart. Healthy heart rate shows **long-range power-law correlations (α ≈ 1.0, "1/f")** characteristic of systems far from equilibrium, whereas **congestive heart failure breaks down the correlation** (α drifts toward 0.5 random or 1.5 Brownian). Healthy ≠ maximally random and ≠ maximally regular — disease *reduces* the fractal complexity. The same fractal/LRTC framework was extended to **human gait** (stride-interval LRTC; Hausdorff et al.) and respiration.

**Costa, Goldberger & Peng (2002)** resolved a paradox: single-scale entropy (e.g. sample entropy) wrongly rates *random* pathological signals as "more complex" than healthy long-range-correlated ones. Their **Multiscale Entropy (MSE)** coarse-grains the series at scales 1…τ_max and computes sample entropy at each scale; the **complexity is the entropy integrated/summed over scales**. Healthy dynamics retain high entropy across *many* scales (high MSE); both white noise (entropy collapses at coarse scales) and over-regular pathological signals (low entropy everywhere) score low. MSE therefore operationalizes **"complexity peaks for structured long-range-correlated signals between order and randomness"** — exactly the edge-of-chaos / Maximum-Effective-Complexity intuition.

## Criticality tests & critiques (artifacts)

The single most transferable *caution* in this field:

- **A power law is necessary, not sufficient.** **Touboul & Destexhe (2017, PRE 95:012413; also 2010 PLoS ONE)** showed that **non-critical stochastic processes can generate clean power-law avalanche distributions and even "universal" scaling**. They argue the decisive test is the **crackling-noise exponent relation** (below): the independently-fit exponents must *agree* with the relation, otherwise the power law is an artifact, not criticality.
- **The crackling-noise exponent relation (Sethna, Dahmen & Myers 2001).** In a genuinely critical system the size exponent τ, duration exponent α_dur, and the size-vs-duration exponent 1/σνz are **not independent**; they must satisfy
  **(α_dur − 1)/(τ − 1) = 1/σνz** (= ⟨S⟩(T) ∝ T^{1/σνz}).
  Plus the **average avalanche shape collapses** onto one universal function. Passing this *joint* test (Friedman 2012) is the modern standard.
- **Subsampling bias.** Recording only a fraction of units biases naive estimators. **Wilting & Priesemann (2018, Nat. Commun. 9:2325)** derived the **multistep-regression (MR) estimator** of the branching ratio that is *consistent under subsampling*; applied in vivo, it finds m slightly *below* 1 — a "reverberating," near-but-not-at-critical regime — overturning naive m≈1 estimates. Lesson: estimator bias from partial observation can manufacture (or destroy) apparent criticality.
- **Finite-size / fitting rigor.** Use **Clauset–Shalizi–Newman (2009)** maximum-likelihood power-law fitting + goodness-of-fit + likelihood-ratio comparison vs. lognormal/exponential alternatives, rather than eyeballing a log-log slope. (Many early avalanche claims failed when re-tested this way.)

## Relevance to this project

The project's `main_findings.md` already (a) fits power-law decay exponents (β), (b) uses a near-independent Hurst axis, (c) computes a data-side complexity scalar (Rényi dimension D_q of n-gram statistics), and (d) hypothesizes an edge-of-chaos strip with maximal effective complexity and best (length-)generalization. The neuro/physio literature offers four concrete upgrades:

1. **Add DFA/MFDFA as a second, trend-robust Hurst estimator.** It is the field-standard LRTC method, gives α directly comparable to your Hurst axis, and MFDFA's singularity-width is a new complexity axis orthogonal to β and D_q. Cheap to run on token streams or activation traces.
2. **Adopt MSE as the direct test of "Maximum Effective Complexity at the edge of chaos."** MSE was *designed* to peak for structured-LRTC signals and dip for both random and over-regular signals — the exact shape you predict across the (β, γ) plane. A clean prediction: MSE(β, γ) is maximized along your emergent/edge strip. This is more on-target than D_q (which is monotone in structure, not peaked).
3. **Borrow the crackling exponent-relation as a falsifiable criticality test.** Your codebase already prizes committed-in-advance, programmatically-checked predictions. The relation (α_dur−1)/(τ−1)=1/σνz lets you fit two exponents and *predict* a third — a far stronger edge-of-chaos claim than any single threshold, and it slots straight into `verify_predictions.py`.
4. **Inherit the criticality caution.** `main_findings.md` already wisely notes that calling a soft boundary a "phase transition" would be "physics-language abuse." The neuro literature is the cautionary case study: decades of "the brain is critical" claims were tempered by Touboul-Destexhe (power laws ≠ criticality) and Wilting-Priesemann (subsampling bias → reverberating, *near*-critical). Any edge-of-chaos claim in this project should require (i) multiple independent signatures, (ii) a passed exponent-relation, and (iii) explicit ruling-out of finite-size/sampling artifacts via MLE fitting (Clauset 2009).
