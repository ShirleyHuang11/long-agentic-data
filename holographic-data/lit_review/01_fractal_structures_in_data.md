## Overview

A recurring empirical fact about natural sequence data — and language in particular — is that its statistical structure is **scale-free**: correlations between tokens do not decay on a single characteristic length but persist as a **power law** across many orders of magnitude, and the "amount of new structure" per unit of context shrinks slowly (sub-exponentially) rather than saturating at a finite memory horizon. This scale-invariance has been described, with overlapping vocabularies, by four research communities:

1. **Statistical physics of text** (1990s–2010s): long-range correlations, 1/f noise, mutual-information power laws, and the Hilberg/Dębowski excess-entropy program.
2. **Quantitative linguistics**: Zipf's law, Heaps' law, Taylor's law, and word burstiness.
3. **Criticality / formal-language theory**: power-law correlations as the signature of being at a critical point, generated by hierarchical (context-free) grammars — the Lin–Tegmark lineage and the Cagnetta–Sclocchi–Wyart "Random Hierarchy Model" lineage.
4. **Deep-learning scaling theory** (2020–2025): connecting the data's fractal/correlation structure to neural scaling-law exponents, length generalization, and the fractal geometry of a trained model's own next-token loss (Alabdulmohsin et al.; Du & Tanaka-Ishii).

The unifying object is a **pair of exponents**: one governing how fast *correlations* decay with separation, and one governing how fast the *conditional entropy / excess entropy* changes with context length. This project's β and γ are direct instances of exactly this pair, which is why this body of work is the right backdrop.

## Seminal works

**Long-range correlations & 1/f noise in text (statistical physics).**
The foundational empirical claim — that letter/word sequences in literary text carry correlations decaying as a power law out to hundreds of symbols — was made by **Ebeling & Pöschel (1994/1995)** (entropy per letter ∼ n^(−1/2); mutual information decaying as a power law) and **Ebeling & Neiman (1995)**, and developed via 1/f-spectrum analyses by **Schenkel, Zhang & Zhang (1993)**. **Montemurro & Pury (2002)** ("Long-range fractal correlations in literary corpora") quantified the fractal scaling of long-range correlations in English literature. These papers established the template the whole field still uses: map text to a numeric series, measure how a fluctuation/entropy statistic scales with window size, read off a scaling exponent.

**Mutual-information power law & the Hilberg program.**
**Hilberg (1990)** conjectured that the entropy of a length-n block of natural language is *not* H·n + const but contains a sub-extensive term ∼ n^β with β<1, i.e. the **excess entropy / mutual information between two adjacent blocks grows as a power of block length** — a sharp qualitative break from any finite-state (Markov) source, where it grows only logarithmically. **Łukasz Dębowski** turned this into a rigorous theory across two decades: *Excess entropy in natural language* (2011), *Hilberg Exponents* (2014, arXiv:1403.1757), and *The Relaxed Hilberg Conjecture* (J. Quant. Linguistics, 2015), connecting power-law mutual information to vocabulary growth (Herdan/Heaps) through his "Santa Fe process" model of texts that "repeatedly describe a random reality."

**Criticality & formal languages.**
**Lin & Tegmark (2017), "Critical Behavior in Physics and Probabilistic Formal Languages"** (Entropy 19(7):299; arXiv:1606.06737) is the conceptual keystone for an "edge-of-chaos" framing. They prove that **probabilistic regular grammars give exponentially decaying mutual information**, whereas **probabilistic context-free / hierarchical grammars can give power-law decay** — i.e. long-range correlations are a generic consequence of *recursive hierarchical generation*, the same mechanism that produces power laws in turbulence and inflationary cosmology. This directly motivates the idea that data with maximal exploitable long-range structure sits at a special (critical) point in a parameter family.

**Zipf, Heaps, burstiness.**
**Zipf (1949)** (rank-frequency power law) and **Heaps/Herdan** (sublinear vocabulary growth) are the classic statistical laws. The modern, statistically careful synthesis is **Altmann & Gerlach (2016), "Statistical Laws in Linguistics"** (arXiv:1502.03296), which stresses that naive i.i.d. fitting fails because real text has **Taylor's-law fluctuations and word burstiness** — long-memory effects that make variances far larger than independence would predict (**Altmann, Pierrehumbert & Motter, 2009**, on burstiness; **Piantadosi, 2014**, critical review of Zipf).

**Fractal dimension from data → scaling exponent.**
**Sharma & Kaplan (2022), "A Neural Scaling Law from the Dimension of the Data Manifold"** (JMLR 23; arXiv:2004.10802) is the seminal bridge from data geometry to learning: if a network effectively regresses on a data manifold of intrinsic dimension d, the loss-vs-resources exponent is α ≈ 4/d. This is the prototype for "a single geometric/fractal property of the data predicts a scaling exponent," which is the logical shape of this project's α_D = γ/(2β).

## Recent advances (2023–2025)

**Alabdulmohsin, Tran & Dehghani (2024), "Fractal Patterns May Illuminate the Success of Next-Token Prediction"** (arXiv:2402.01825; earlier titled "…May Unravel the Intelligence in…"). The most directly relevant recent paper. They treat the **stream of next-token log-probabilities (self-information / surprisal) under an LLM as a 1-D signal**, take its integrated/increment process, and measure three fractal descriptors: a **self-similarity (Hölder) exponent**, a **Hurst parameter H ≈ 0.7** (long-range dependent; the "Joseph effect"), and a fat-tail ("Noah") exponent. The headline qualitative claims: language is **self-similar with no characteristic context length**, and **long-range dependent**; short-scope structure mirrors long-scope structure, which they argue is *why* next-token prediction transfers across granularities. Practically, they report that **tiny cross-model differences in fractal parameters predict downstream performance better than bits-per-byte perplexity alone**. (Exact exponent tables are in the full PDF; H≈0.7 and the BPB-vs-fractal claim are from the abstract/body and worth re-checking before quoting precisely.)

**Du & Tanaka-Ishii (2025), "Correlation Dimension of Auto-Regressive Large Language Models"** (arXiv:2510.21258; NeurIPS 2025 poster). Introduces the **correlation dimension** (a Grassberger–Procaccia fractal-geometric measure of self-similarity) applied to an LLM's trajectory in representation/probability space. Findings: correlation dimension **reveals three distinct phases during pretraining**, tracks **context-dependent complexity**, **predicts hallucination tendency**, and **detects text degeneration** (repetition, incoherence) that low perplexity misses. Robust to 4-bit quantization and works for both Transformer and Mamba. This is the closest existing work to using a *fractal complexity scalar* as a phase/quality diagnostic — methodologically parallel to this project's Rényi-dimension diagnostic.

**Chen et al. (2025), "L²M: Mutual Information Scaling Law for Long-Context Language Modeling"** (arXiv:2503.04725; NeurIPS 2025). Establishes a **bipartite mutual-information power law I ∼ L^β in natural language** (PG19), distinct from and scaling independently of two-point mutual information, and derives the **"L²M condition"**: a model's history-state (KV cache / SSM state) size must grow at least as fast as this power law for effective long-context modeling. Validated on Transformers and state-space models. This is the most rigorous 2025 statement that **a data-side power-law exponent dictates an architecture-side capacity requirement** — extremely close in spirit to this project's β and to the Transformer-vs-Mamba length-generalization comparison in Result 11.

**Cagnetta, Sclocchi, Petrini, Favero, Tomasini, Wyart and collaborators (2024–2025): the Random Hierarchy Model (RHM) lineage.**
- **Cagnetta, Petrini, Tomasini, Favero & Wyart (2024), "How Deep Networks Learn Sparse and Hierarchical Data: the Sparse Random Hierarchy Model."**
- **Cagnetta & Wyart (2024), "Towards a theory of how the structure of language is acquired by deep neural networks"** (NeurIPS 2024; arXiv:2406.00048): in the RHM (an ensemble of probabilistic context-free grammars), **token–token correlations are computed analytically and decay with a power law in separation**; a **finite training set limits the resolution of correlations to an effective range that grows with dataset size**, yielding scaling laws for how much grammar the model has learned. This is the cleanest existing formalization of "data-limited effective correlation range," and is the most direct theoretical sibling of α_D.
- **Sclocchi, Favero & Wyart (2025)** on diffusion / hierarchical compositional data, and **(2025) "Scaling Laws and Representation Learning in Simple Hierarchical Languages: Transformers vs. Convolutional Architectures"** (arXiv:2505.07070): predicts and verifies that architectures matched to the generative locality (CNNs) scale faster than Transformers on hierarchical data — an architecture-vs-structure result paralleling this project's Mamba findings.

**Dębowski (2025), "From Zipf's Law to Neural Scaling through Heaps' Law and Hilberg's Hypothesis"** (arXiv:2512.13491). A clean deductive chain: **Zipf ⇒ Heaps ⇒ Hilberg ⇒ neural scaling law**, illustrated on the Santa Fe process that satisfies all four laws. The most explicit recent statement that **the neural scaling exponent is downstream of the data's power-law statistics** — the same logical move as α_D = γ/(2β).

**Other 2023–2025 strands.** Multifractal analyses of language continue (e.g., word-adjacency networks, sentence-length series — *Multifractal analysis of sentence lengths*, arXiv:1212.3171 and successors), and **Bahri, Dyer, Kaplan, Lee & Sharma (2024), "Explaining Neural Scaling Laws"** (PNAS 121) plus **Brill (2024), "Neural Scaling Laws Rooted in the Data Distribution"** (arXiv:2412.07942, percolation model) refine the data-distribution origin of scaling exponents.

## Methods & metrics

| Metric | What it measures | Estimator | Exponent symbol |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Autocorrelation / 2-point correlation C(d)** | how token similarity decays with separation d | C(d) ∼ d^(−β); fit on log–log | **β (this project)** |
| **Mutual information I(d)** between symbols d apart | model-free dependence; distinguishes Markov (exp decay) from hierarchical (power law) | plug-in / compression estimators | power-law exponent ↔ Hilberg β |
| **Excess entropy / bipartite MI I(L)** | total predictable information in a length-L block | block-entropy extrapolation; LLM-based estimators (L²M) | Hilberg β |
| **Conditional entropy Hₙ vs context n** | how much uncertainty remains given n tokens | entropy-rate convergence | **γ (this project): Hₙ ∼ n^(−γ)** |
| **Hurst exponent H** | long-range dependence of an integrated series; H>0.5 ⇒ persistent LRD | R/S analysis; **DFA**; wavelets | H (Alabdulmohsin H≈0.7) |
| **(Multi)fractal dimension; MF-DFA spectrum f(α), h(q)** | scale-invariance, possibly with a *spectrum* of exponents | **MF-DFA**, wavelet leaders | h(q), f(α) |
| **Correlation dimension** | self-similarity of a trajectory in state space | Grassberger–Procaccia | D₂ (Du & Tanaka-Ishii) |
| **Rényi / generalized dimension D_q** | multifractal scaling of n-gram measure | partition-function / box-counting | D_q (this project's data diagnostic) |
| **Data-manifold intrinsic dimension d** | geometry the network regresses on | nearest-neighbor / MLE estimators | α ≈ 4/d (Sharma–Kaplan) |

Practical cautions repeatedly raised: **finite-size and nonstationarity bias** (DFA was invented to handle trends; MF-DFA to separate genuine multifractality from fat tails — see Kantelhardt et al. 2002); **isolated singularities can masquerade as multifractality** (arXiv:2004.03319); and Zipf/Heaps fits are corrupted by burstiness/Taylor fluctuations unless modeled (Altmann & Gerlach). For β and γ specifically, the key risk is **conflating a fat-tailed marginal (Noah effect) with genuine long memory (Joseph effect)** — they are distinct exponents and should be estimated separately.

## Open questions / debates

1. **Is power-law correlation "real" or an artifact of mixing/nonstationarity?** Heterogeneous topic mixtures can mimic long-range correlations (cf. *On the origin of long-range correlations in texts*, PNAS 2012, Altmann et al.). Disentangling intrinsic long memory from compositional heterogeneity is unsettled — and is exactly the β-vs-noise question this project's γ knob probes.
2. **One exponent or a spectrum?** Whether language is genuinely *multi*fractal (a nontrivial f(α)) or effectively *mono*fractal is debated; translations and genres shift the spectrum, suggesting it is not universal.
3. **Does criticality help learning, or just describe data?** The edge-of-chaos computation literature (below) argues critical dynamics maximize computational capacity, but whether *critical data* maximizes *learnability / generalization* is largely a hypothesis — precisely this project's thesis.
4. **Which exponent controls scaling?** Sharma–Kaplan (manifold d), Bahri et al. (percolation), Brill (data distribution), and Dębowski (Zipf→Hilberg) give *different* data-side origins for the same neural scaling exponent. There is no consensus reduction; α_D = γ/(2β) is a specific, testable competitor.
5. **Architecture sensitivity.** L²M and the RHM-CNN/Transformer results imply the *same* data exponent imposes *different* costs on different architectures — open as to whether a single data-side critical point is optimal across architectures, or architecture-relative.

## Relevance to this project (explicit ties to β, γ, Hurst, α_D)

This project's two exponents are textbook instances of the two exponents this literature has measured for thirty years:

- **β (power-law decay of token–token correlations, C(d) ∼ d^(−β)).** This is exactly the long-range-correlation exponent of Ebeling–Pöschel / Montemurro–Pury, the mutual-information exponent of Lin–Tegmark (regular ⇒ exponential, hierarchical ⇒ power-law), and the analytically-derived RHM correlation exponent of **Cagnetta & Wyart (2406.00048)**. In the KV-retrieval task, β literally parametrizes a power-law retrieval-distance distribution p(d) ∝ d^(−(β+1)) — i.e. you are *synthesizing* data with a tunable correlation exponent, the controlled analogue of what those papers *measured* in natural text. **L²M (2503.04725)** then says β should dictate the required history-state size — a ready-made prediction for why Mamba (growing SSM state) length-generalizes better than a fixed-window Transformer (Result 11).

- **γ (Hₙ ∼ n^(−γ), conditional-entropy decay with context).** This is the differential form of the **Hilberg/Dębowski excess-entropy** statement: a sub-extensive entropy term ⇔ slowly-decaying conditional entropy ⇔ power-law mutual information. Dębowski (2512.13491) makes the chain Heaps ⇒ Hilberg ⇒ neural-scaling explicit, so γ is the project's controllable Hilberg exponent.

- **Hurst exponent.** **Alabdulmohsin et al. (2402.01825)** measure H≈0.7 on the LLM surprisal series; H is a monotone repackaging of the same long-memory information that β and γ carry (for a long-range-dependent series, the autocorrelation exponent and H are linked, e.g. C(d) ∼ d^(2H−2)). Reporting an H for the project's sequences — and for the *model's surprisal stream* à la Alabdulmohsin — would connect β/γ to the most-cited recent fractal-language result and provide an independent cross-check.

- **α_D = γ/(2β) (data-limited scaling exponent).** Structurally identical to the field's central move — *a single composite of data-side power-law exponents predicts a learning/scaling exponent*: Sharma–Kaplan's α≈4/d, Dębowski's Zipf→Hilberg→scaling chain, and especially **Cagnetta & Wyart's "effective correlation range that grows with dataset size."** That RHM result is the closest theoretical relative of α_D and the natural place to look for a first-principles derivation (or refutation) of the γ/(2β) form. Note the project's own `main_findings.md` already finds α_D is **not** a single threshold separating phases (Result 2) — consistent with the open debate (#4) that no single data exponent cleanly governs the boundary; the boundary there is better described as soft and linear in (log β, γ).

- **Edge-of-chaos / maximal effective complexity.** The hypothesis that a special (β, γ) strip carries the most *exploitable* long-range structure is the data-side analogue of **Bertschinger & Natschläger (2004), "Real-Time Computation at the Edge of Chaos in RNNs"** (Neural Computation) and **Boedecker et al. (2012)** on echo-state networks: computational capacity peaks at the order↔chaos transition. Combined with Lin–Tegmark (power-law correlations = criticality of hierarchical sources) and Du & Tanaka-Ishii's "three phases" / correlation-dimension diagnostic, this gives a principled reason to expect a *critical strip* where data is maximally learnable and length-generalization is best — the project's central claim. The project's Rényi-D_q data diagnostic (Result 8) is methodologically the same family as the correlation-dimension and MF-DFA tools above, lending it external validity.

**Single most useful connection:** Cagnetta & Wyart (NeurIPS 2024, arXiv:2406.00048) — analytic power-law token correlations in a hierarchical grammar plus a *dataset-size-limited effective correlation range* — is the theoretical template most likely to yield a first-principles account of α_D = γ/(2β) and to ground the edge-of-chaos/maximal-complexity strip in a generative model rather than only in measured exponents.
