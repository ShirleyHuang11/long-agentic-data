## S1. Early nitrate-film vault preservation: practices, risks, and access constraints
Cellulose nitrate film defined cinema’s first half-century but came with an unforgiving risk profile. Chemically unstable and highly combustible, nitrate demanded environmental control and segregation from other media. The National Film Preservation Foundation notes nitrate film stock saw commercial use through the early 1950s, after which acetate “safety” film took over—an implicit acknowledgement that nitrate’s hazards and decay made it untenable long-term [https://www.filmpreservation.org/preservation-basics/nitrate-degradation].

Safety was engineered into the built environment and daily routines. A National Archives blog recounts how, in fires, films stored in closed-front, compartmented racks (Bradley racks) sometimes survived even absent sprinklers—evidence of how vault design aimed to compartmentalize risk and contain ignition [https://unwritten-record.blogs.archives.gov/2018/12/10/film-preservation-101-is-nitrate-film-really-dangerous/]. Handling guidance codified the separation of nitrate from “safety” stocks and emphasized careful management of unstable reels. Kodak’s storage/handling recommendations highlight that nitrate must be managed separately and with PPE-level caution, including admonitions not to mix it with safety stock destined for silver recovery and to treat deteriorated nitrate with heightened precautions [https://www.kodak.com/en/motion/page/storage-and-handling-of-processed-nitrate-film/]. At an even more procedural level, FIAF’s handling manual recommends examining every film upon arrival and recording a technical condition, followed by tests before storage—underscoring proactive inspection as a first barrier against unnoticed chemical decomposition [https://www.fiafnet.org/images/tinyUpload/Publications/FIAF-Bookshop/Handling_Storage_transport_of_Cellulose_Nitrate_film-RED.pdf].

What issues did these practices address?
- Deterioration: Early decomposition (stickiness, ambering, powdering) and heat sensitivity. The aim was to slow chemical reactions via cool, dry, ventilated storage and to isolate deteriorating reels [https://www.filmpreservation.org/preservation-basics/nitrate-degradation].
- Fire/explosion risk: Vault design (compartmented racks, fire-rated rooms) and handling protocols sought to prevent ignition and limit spread—practical design responses to nitrate’s combustion characteristics [https://unwritten-record.blogs.archives.gov/2018/12/10/film-preservation-101-is-nitrate-film-really-dangerous/; https://www.kodak.com/en/motion/page/storage-and-handling-of-processed-nitrate-film/].
- Accessibility constraints: Because of the added risk, projecting or even inspecting nitrate required extra time and controls. UCLA reports prepping a nitrate film for screening takes two to three times longer than prepping acetate—time that reflects specialized handling and safety procedures and thereby constrains public access and programming [https://newsroom.ucla.edu/stories/taking-pre-1950s-nitrate-film-from-the-vault-to-the-screen].

Contextual anchors include the 1889 commercial introduction of nitrate film by Eastman Kodak in still photography, which set up nitrate’s long dominance and the eventual need for vault-centric mitigation [https://www.chicagohistory.org/preserving-nitrate-negatives/]. Institutions like the George Eastman Museum emphasize why purpose-built vaults were constructed to “hold and keep nitrate film from deteriorating over the years,” reinforcing the centrality of environmental control [https://www.eastman.org/blog/nitrate-film-beginning]. The BFI’s overview likewise frames nitrate’s succession by safer stocks, a transition that would gradually rebalance safety and access [https://www.bfi.org.uk/features/all-about-nitrate-film].

Bottom line: Nitrate-era preservation was about containment and survival—minimizing combustion risk and slowing decay. The trade-off was access: safety measures added time and complexity to preparation, projection, and duplication.

## S2. From nitrate to “safety” film: reduced flammability, new decay, and access gains
“Safety” film—cellulose acetate—began supplanting nitrate in the 1920s (first in still photography) and across motion pictures by mid-century. Conservation literature describes multiple acetate variants produced after 1925 (diacetate, acetate propionate, etc.) and treats acetate as the canonical “safety” base that replaced nitrate in standard use [https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conservation_and_restoration_of_film]. The BFI explains the sequence succinctly: nitrate was succeeded by acetate and later by more stable polyester stocks [https://www.bfi.org.uk/features/all-about-nitrate-film].

Which problems did this stage solve?
- Fire hazard: Acetate’s non-nitrate base reduced flammability, lowering the catastrophic risk profile in storage and projection and enabling wider exhibition and duplication without specialized nitrate procedures [https://www.bfi.org.uk/features/all-about-nitrate-film].

What new issues emerged?
- Acetate decay (e.g., vinegar syndrome): While much safer in terms of combustion, acetate introduced its own deterioration modes—shrinkage, embrittlement, acetate hydrolysis—recognized in conservation practice and literature that catalogues these agents and types of deterioration [https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conservation_and_restoration_of_film].

What changed about access?
- Projection and duplication became more routine for “safety” elements, eliminating much of nitrate’s procedural overhead. UCLA’s comparison—nitrate prep taking 2–3× longer than acetate for screenings—illustrates the practical access gains in moving away from nitrate [https://newsroom.ucla.edu/stories/taking-pre-1950s-nitrate-film-from-the-vault-to-the-screen].

Net effect: Safety film rebalanced the equation toward more usable, less dangerous collections, at the cost of new preservation tasks (monitoring and mitigating acetate decay) and continued dependence on photochemical duplication to manage wear and generational loss.

## S3. Digitization and the rise of storage architectures designed for access at scale
Digitization reframed the core problem: from substrate care to data lifecycle management. Rather than projecting or splicing original reels, high-resolution scans create digital surrogates for access and restoration while physical elements remain stabilized in storage. The operational center of gravity moves to storage architecture, metadata, and workflow control.

## S4. AI-assisted digital restoration pipelines: capabilities and cautions
With high-resolution scans in hand, restoration pipelines now incorporate AI to boost both speed and quality in tasks that were once painstakingly manual:
- Automated defect repair: AI can identify and correct scratches, specks, and color loss across frames, yielding cleaner images with reduced human retouching [https://beverlyboy.com/film-technology/pixels-preservation-ais-takeover-of-film-archiving/].
- Frame reconstruction: Generative approaches can interpolate or reconstruct missing/damaged frames, improving continuity and watchability where physical loss occurred [https://pulitzercenter.org/stories/saving-cinema-ais-starring-role-preserving-film-archives].
- Throughput and stabilization: Accounts of AI-driven workflows report frame-by-frame cleaning and stabilization for classic films, accelerating restoration compared to purely manual approaches [https://beverlyboy.com/film-technology/pixels-preservation-ais-takeover-of-film-archiving/].
- Assistive roles across the pipeline: Some vendors portray AI as supporting not just cleanup but also digitization workflows and quality analysis—though specifics vary and require careful validation [https://vitrina.ai/blog/role-ai-restoring-preserving-film-history/; https://www.numberanalytics.com/blog/film-preservation-in-the-digital-era].

What problems does AI address?
- Labor and time: It compresses repetitive frame-by-frame tasks, enabling larger backlogs to be processed sooner and expanding what archives can make accessible.
- Quality and consistency: Algorithmic defect detection yields more uniform baseline cleaning before human fine-tuning.

What are the limits and risks?
- Authenticity and interpretation: Automated restoration can unintentionally alter look, tone, or meaning—e.g., oversmoothing textures or inventing detail when reconstructing frames. Both practitioners and commentators emphasize the necessity of human supervision and ethical guardrails [https://beverlyboy.com/film-technology/pixels-preservation-ais-takeover-of-film-archiving/; https://domitor.org/artificial-intelligence-film-restoration-and-early-cinema-notes-from-amia-2022/].
- Modality clarity: The community distinguishes photochemical restoration (original media processes), digital restoration (non-AI software tools), and AI-driven methods. Keeping these categories clear helps curators, scholars, and audiences understand what changes were made and why [https://domitor.org/artificial-intelligence-film-restoration-and-early-cinema-notes-from-amia-2022/].

In short: AI extends digital restoration’s reach—especially for scratched, dirty, or incomplete elements—while heightening the importance of curatorial oversight and documentation.

FAA’s Day–Night Average Sound Level (DNL) is the policy-standard descriptor and 65 dB DNL is the historical significant impact threshold under policy; however, FAA is actively reviewing its noise policy, highlighting the need to present supplemental metrics to communicate effects, especially for UAM/eVTOL [https://www.faa.gov/regulations_policies/policy_guidance/noise/basics; https://www.faa.gov/regulations_policies/policy_guidance/noise/history; https://www.faa.gov/noisepolicyreview; https://www.atac.com/wp-content/uploads/2021/03/White_Paper_Noise_supplemental_20210216.pdf].
- For UAM/eVTOL, AEDT is being extended to accommodate new vehicles; comparative studies indicate AEDT’s limitations for complex rotorcraft/aAM acoustics, so a calibrated approach (e.g., Volpe AAM models) and UAM-focused frameworks should be used where appropriate [https://rosap.ntl.bts.gov/view/dot/63310/dot_63310_DS1.pdf; https://ntrs.nasa.gov/api/citations/20210015134/downloads/IN21_AAM_v12.pdf; https://research.tudelft.nl/files/213838959/lima-pereira-et-al-2024-a-preliminary-design-framework-for-performance-and-noise-assessment-of-urban-air-mobility.pdf].
- Rigour requires uncertainty quantification and validation. AEDT has documented uncertainty quantification; FAA/Volpe use AEDT for federally required environmental reviews, balancing NAS efficiency/safety with community noise, and support ICAO policymaking—offering a governance model for transparent, defensible results [https://aedt.faa.gov/Documents/AEDT2b_Uncertainty%20Quantification_Report.pdf; https://www.volpe.dot.gov/news/faa-makes-environmentally-responsible-decisions-using-aviation-environmental-design-tool].
- The recommended workflow couples: (1) mission-standardized operational modeling (AEDT), (2) integrated LCA add-on for upstream/downstream impacts, (3) ICAO-aligned safety SPIs with uncertainty bands, and (4) cost per seat‑km with benchmarking-style documentation. Outputs are rendered as a scorecard with scenario ranges and model provenance.

## Main Body

## S1. Scope and principles: the framework’s four pillars anchored in aviation practice
Takeaway: A credible benchmark must inherit rigor from the aviation community’s established tools and policies rather than start from scratch.

- Environmental operational modeling: AEDT is the FAA’s integrated software to model aircraft performance in space and time to estimate fuel consumption, emissions, noise, and air quality consequences, and it is the required tool for environmental review of airport and airspace projects [https://aedt.faa.gov/; https://www.faa.gov/about/office_org/headquarters_offices/apl/aee/research/models].
- Policy and governance: FAA/Volpe indicate AEDT supports federally required reviews and policy development, balancing system safety/efficiency with community noise concerns, and providing inputs to ICAO’s standards processes—highlighting how tool outputs translate into decisions [https://www.volpe.dot.gov/news/faa-makes-environmentally-responsible-decisions-using-aviation-environmental-design-tool].
- Safety performance: ICAO’s safety performance framework sets high-level focus areas and guides the use of SPIs, which are essential to normalize “safety margins” across diverse architectures [https://www4.icao.int/ganpportal/GIPEGSafetyPerformanceFrameworkAnalysis].
- Noise metrics: FAA defines DNL as the standard descriptor for environmental assessments; historically, 65 dB DNL has been the threshold for significant noise impact. FAA is in an active Noise Policy Review, signaling openness to metric evolution or supplementation [https://www.faa.gov/regulations_policies/policy_guidance/noise/basics; https://www.faa.gov/regulations_policies/policy_guidance/noise/history; https://www.faa.gov/noisepolicyreview; https://www.faa.gov/noisepolicyreview/summary-of-comments].
- Economic benchmarking discipline: FAA–EUROCONTROL’s international benchmarking report compares operational and cost-efficiency performance, aligned with ICAO GANP performance indicators, demonstrating a robust method for transparent, comparative performance reporting [https://www.faa.gov/about/office_org/headquarters_offices/ato/service_units/systemops/perf_analysis/benchmarking].

Design principle: unify these anchors into an aircraft-concept benchmark with four pillars—Climate, Safety, Noise, Economics—plus cross-cutting uncertainty and maturity.

## S2. Life-cycle carbon: standardizing LCA across architectures
Takeaway: Upstream and downstream processes can reorder climate rankings; LCA must be integrated into conceptual evaluation with consistent boundaries.

- Conceptual design integration: Studies show how to embed LCA in conceptual aircraft design and establish baseline metrics for comparative evaluation—providing a template for embedding life-cycle reasoning early [https://www.mdpi.com/2226-4310/11/1/101].
- Stage dominance: LCA work on an A320-class aircraft identifies life-cycle stage contributions, enabling focus on high-leverage phases and preventing tank-to-wake-only comparisons [https://arc.aiaa.org/doi/full/10.2514/6.2022-1028].
- Industry guidance: Aerospace LCA framework documents provide standardized approaches, inventory choices, and reporting expectations, facilitating cross-organization comparability [https://www.iaeg.com/binaries/content/assets/iaeg/2025/wg12/wg12-lca-frmwk-document-_v1_final.pdf].

Proposed method
- Functional unit: gCO2e per seat‑km on standardized missions (e.g., 200 km urban, 500 km regional, 1500 km short‑haul).
- Boundaries: cradle‑to‑grave (materials and manufacturing; energy supply chain including grid mix or H2 pathway; in‑use operations; maintenance; end‑of‑life).
- Coupling with AEDT: Use AEDT for operational fuel burn/energy and emissions to generate high‑fidelity in‑use data (including climb/approach profiles), and supplement with an LCA module for upstream/downstream stages [https://aedt.faa.gov/].
- Scenarios: grid carbon intensity ranges, hydrogen production pathways, battery replacement cycles, and reserve energy policies.
- Output: Median gCO2e/seat‑km with uncertainty bands and scenario ranges.

## S3. Safety: normalizing margins and uncertainty across novel concepts
Takeaway: Use ICAO’s safety performance framing and non-deterministic simulation to turn disparate safety features into comparable indicators with confidence ranges.

- Framework anchor: ICAO’s safety performance framework identifies high-level focus areas and provides a schema for SPIs—metrics of safety performance that can be mapped to concept-specific risks (e.g., energy system failure rates, autorotation outcomes for eVTOL, redundancy coverage) [https://www4.icao.int/ganpportal/GIPEGSafetyPerformanceFrameworkAnalysis].
- Uncertainty-driven quantification: NASA’s methodology applies uncertainty analysis and non-deterministic simulations to estimate performance where full empirical datasets are unavailable—an approach essential for early-stage aircraft safety margin estimation [https://ntrs.nasa.gov/api/citations/20220017737/downloads/ApplicationofFramework_11292022.pdf].

Proposed method
- Define SPIs per concept category and mission (e.g., probability of loss of thrust, energy reserve margin compliance, capability to meet alternate/contingency profiles).
- Quantify with simulation ensembles and fault trees; report as SPI distributions (e.g., 5th/50th/95th percentiles).
- Track maturity: a “technology readiness–to–safety readiness” mapping that records the evidence base behind SPI estimates (test hours, representative trials).
- Integrate operational context: Use FAA benchmarking discipline to separate intrinsic vehicle safety from ATM/ANSP operational influences when interpreting safety outcomes [https://www.faa.gov/about/office_org/headquarters_offices/ato/service_units/systemops/perf_analysis/benchmarking].

## S4. Community noise: policy-standard metrics plus supplemental descriptors for UAM/eVTOL
Takeaway: Combine policy-standard DNL with supplemental metrics (SEL, event counts, time-of-day weightings) and use calibrated models for rotorcraft/UAM.

- Standards baseline: FAA defines DNL as the standard descriptor for environmental noise in aviation; historically, 65 dB DNL has served as a significance threshold in policy analysis [https://www.faa.gov/regulations_policies/policy_guidance/noise/basics; https://www.faa.gov/regulations_policies/policy_guidance/noise/history].
- Policy evolution: FAA’s ongoing Noise Policy Review and associated comment summaries evidence a live debate on metrics and thresholds—strengthening the case for offering supplemental metrics [https://www.faa.gov/noisepolicyreview; https://www.faa.gov/noisepolicyreview/summary-of-comments].
- Supplemental metrics: Professional guidance recommends supplemental noise metrics to communicate effects (e.g., number above a threshold, event frequency, evening sensitivity) [https://www.atac.com/wp-content/uploads/2021/03/White_Paper_Noise_supplemental_20210216.pdf]. Peer-reviewed work catalogs the taxonomy of single-event and cumulative metrics, aiding selection [https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10203677/].
- Tools: AEDT models noise exposure and is used in FAA environmental review; its scope is being expanded to include UAM vehicles [https://aedt.faa.gov/; https://rosap.ntl.bts.gov/view/dot/63310/dot_63310_DS1.pdf].
- UAM specificity: Comparative modeling shows AEDT can be limited for advanced rotorcraft/AAM tonal/time-domain acoustics, motivating calibrated use or alternate models for UAM [https://ntrs.nasa.gov/api/citations/20210015134/downloads/IN21_AAM_v12.pdf]. UAM design frameworks demonstrate integrated performance-noise assessments early in design [https://research.tudelft.nl/files/213838959/lima-pereira-et-al-2024-a-preliminary-design-framework-for-performance-and-noise-assessment-of-urban-air-mobility.pdf].
- Community noise procedures: Frameworks exist for analyzing noise impacts of advanced approach/departure procedures, enabling the benchmark to evaluate operational mitigations consistently [https://arc.aiaa.org/doi/10.2514/1.C035100].

Proposed method
- For each mission, compute DNL contour changes via AEDT alongside supplemental metrics: SEL distributions for representative events, number-above thresholds by time-of-day, and exposure of sensitive receptors.
- For UAM, apply AEDT with UAM extensions or calibrated alternative models as indicated by comparative studies; document model selection and calibration basis [https://rosap.ntl.bts.gov/view/dot/63310/dot_63310_DS1.pdf; https://ntrs.nasa.gov/api/citations/20210015134/downloads/IN21_AAM_v12.pdf].
- Present results as both area/population exposure and per-mission event footprints to reflect operational realities.

## S5. Economics: cost per seat‑kilometre with benchmarking discipline
Takeaway: While detailed cost models are aircraft/operator-specific, the reporting discipline used in ATM/airport benchmarking provides a clear template for transparent, comparable cost metrics.

- Benchmarking anchor: FAA–EUROCONTROL’s international benchmarking compares operational and cost-efficiency performance with alignment to ICAO GANP performance indicators—illustrating how to document assumptions, normalize for context, and present trends [https://www.faa.gov/about/office_org/headquarters_offices/ato/service_units/systemops/perf_analysis/benchmarking].
- Airport metrics templates: ACRP documentation outlines common performance metrics spanning capacity, environment, and cost-effectiveness and references standard tools like AEDT—useful for structuring cost reporting categories and linking environmental performance to economics [https://crp.trb.org/acrp0715/wp-content/themes/acrp-child/documents/216/original/acrp_r190.pdf].
- ANSP cost-effectiveness frameworks (EUROCONTROL ACE) detail cost driver decomposition methods; while not aircraft costs, they exemplify transparent cost-structure reporting that can be adapted to aircraft economic benchmarking [https://www.eurocontrol.int/sites/default/files/2024-06/eurocontrol-ace-benchmarking-report-2024-edition.pdf].

Proposed method
- Functional unit: cost per seat‑km by mission (and optionally by stage length bin).
- Cost elements: energy (electricity/H2/Jet-A), maintenance (including propulsion system-specific overhauls), airframe depreciation, insurance, crew, landing/airspace charges, and battery/hydrogen system capital amortization where relevant.
- Documentation: Publish input ranges and normalizations (e.g., capacity factors, utilization, energy price ranges) akin to ATM benchmarking practice; present medians with 5th–95th percentile bands to reflect uncertainty.

## S6. Cross-cutting rigor: mission definitions, uncertainty quantification, and validation
Takeaway: Without standardized missions and quantified uncertainty, comparisons can mislead.

- Mission definitions: Establish standard mission profiles (stage length, reserves, alternates), atmospheric models, and ATM context (e.g., vectoring assumptions) so AEDT outputs are comparable across concepts [https://aedt.faa.gov/].
- Uncertainty quantification: AEDT’s uncertainty quantification report articulates techniques to characterize modeling uncertainty; adopting similar practice across emissions and noise avoids false precision [https://aedt.faa.gov/Documents/AEDT2b_Uncertainty%20Quantification_Report.pdf]. NASA’s non-deterministic approach further supports expressing performance as ranges with confidence [https://ntrs.nasa.gov/api/citations/20220017737/downloads/ApplicationofFramework_11292022.pdf].
- Validation: FAA/Volpe use AEDT as the foundation for federally required reviews and policy development; AEDT noise model validation projects and preliminary real‑world validations underpin credibility, and comparative studies highlight where models require calibration for AAM [https://www.volpe.dot.gov/news/faa-makes-environmentally-responsible-decisions-using-aviation-environmental-design-tool; https://ascent.aero/project/noise-model-validation-for-aedt/; https://ntrs.nasa.gov/api/citations/20210015134/downloads/IN21_AAM_v12.pdf].
- Policy alignment: The framework should track evolving policy (e.g., FAA Noise Policy Review) and ICAO environmental/safety processes so that outputs map to decision-relevant metrics and thresholds [https://www.faa.gov/noisepolicyreview; https://www.icao.int/environmental-protection/aircraft-noise].

## S7. The unified benchmark: architecture, workflow, and scorecard
Takeaway: A practical, defensible framework can be built now by coupling established tools and methods, with clearly defined outputs.

Architecture
- Pillars and outputs:
  - Climate: gCO2e/seat‑km with cradle‑to‑grave LCA boundaries; scenario bands.
  - Safety: ICAO‑aligned SPIs with uncertainty ranges; explicit safety margins for reserves/contingencies.
  - Noise: DNL-based exposure deltas plus supplemental metrics (SEL distributions, number‑above‑threshold, evening sensitivity) per mission; UAM‑specific calibrated models where required.
  - Economics: cost per seat‑km with decomposed cost elements and uncertainty bands.
- Cross‑cutting: maturity level (evidence base, testing), uncertainty quantification, and traceable model provenance.

Workflow
1) Define standardized missions and assumptions (payload, seat count, reserves, alternates).
2) Operational modeling in AEDT to generate fuel/energy, in‑use emissions, and noise exposures (calibrated UAM models where indicated) [https://aedt.faa.gov/; https://rosap.ntl.bts.gov/view/dot/63310/dot_63310_DS1.pdf; https://ntrs.nasa.gov/api/citations/20210015134/downloads/IN21_AAM_v12.pdf].
3) LCA augmentation to add upstream/downstream impacts with conceptual design integration [https://www.mdpi.com/2226-4310/11/1/101; https://arc.aiaa.org/doi/full/10.2514/6.2022-1028; https://www.iaeg.com/binaries/content/assets/iaeg/2025/wg12/wg12-lca-frmwk-document-_v1_final.pdf].
4) Safety SPI definition and quantification using ICAO framework and non‑deterministic simulation to produce uncertainty bands [https://www4.icao.int/ganpportal/GIPEGSafetyPerformanceFrameworkAnalysis; https://ntrs.nasa.gov/api/citations/20220017737/downloads/ApplicationofFramework_11292022.pdf].
5) Economics modeling to calculate cost per seat‑km, documented with benchmarking discipline (input transparency, decomposition) [https://www.faa.gov/about/office_org/headquarters_offices/ato/service_units/systemops/perf_analysis/benchmarking; https://crp.trb.org/acrp0715/wp-content/themes/acrp-child/documents/216/original/acrp_r190.pdf].
6) Uncertainty quantification/validation gates using AEDT UQ practices and published validation studies [https://aedt.faa.gov/Documents/AEDT2b_Uncertainty%20Quantification_Report.pdf; https://ascent.aero/project/noise-model-validation-for-aedt/].
7) Scorecard output with ranges, plus a harmonized narrative that maps results to policy metrics (e.g., DNL contours and supplemental metrics; SPIs).

Scoring and weighting
- Multi-criteria approach: Use transparent weighting (e.g., stakeholder-defined) and sensitivity testing. The aeronautics literature supports multi-dimensional assessment across efficiency, eco‑efficiency, and costs, which we adapt to include safety and community noise [https://ieeexplore.ieee.org/document/8792641].
- Report tables: provide both raw metrics and indexed scores by mission, with uncertainty whiskers.

Implementation for different concept classes
- eVTOL/UAM: Adopt UAM‑specific noise models where AEDT is limited, as evidenced by comparative studies; apply near‑urban mission sets and consider vertiport procedures [https://rosap.ntl.bts.gov/view/dot/63310/dot_63310_DS1.pdf; https://ntrs.nasa.gov/api/citations/20210015134/downloads/IN21_AAM_v12.pdf; https://research.tudelft.nl/files/213838959/lima-pereira-et-al-2024-a-preliminary-design-framework-for-performance-and-noise-assessment-of-urban-air-mobility.pdf].
- Regional/short-haul hydrogen and electrics: Emphasize LCA uncertainty around energy pathways and reserves; operational modeling remains AEDT‑anchored [https://aedt.faa.gov/; https://www.mdpi.com/2226-4310/11/1/101].

Illustrative metric table (example structure; values to be computed per project)
- Climate: gCO2e/seat‑km (median; 5–95%).
- Safety: SPI1 (e.g., probability of failure to meet alternate reserve), SPI2 (energy redundancy compliance), SPI3 (loss‑of‑thrust recovery capability); each with 5–95% ranges [https://www4.icao.int/ganpportal/GIPEGSafetyPerformanceFrameworkAnalysis; https://ntrs.nasa.gov/api/citations/20220017737/downloads/ApplicationofFramework_11292022.pdf].
- Noise: Δ population within DNL 65 dB contour, average SEL at key receptors, number‑above‑X per evening hour [https://www.faa.gov/regulations_policies/policy_guidance/noise/basics; https://www.atac.com/wp-content/uploads/2021/03/White_Paper_Noise_supplemental_20210216.pdf].
- Economics: cost per seat‑km (median; 5–95%), with decomposed cost shares [https://www.faa.gov/about/office_org/headquarters_offices/ato/service_units/systemops/perf_analysis/benchmarking; https://crp.trb.org/acrp0715/wp-content/themes/acrp-child/documents/216/original/acrp_r190.pdf].

Governance and transparency
- Provenance: Archive model versions, input datasets, and calibration notes (especially for UAM noise models).
- Policy mapping: Present both standardized policy metrics (DNL) and supplemental descriptors given FAA’s ongoing review [https://www.faa.gov/noisepolicyreview; https://www.faa.gov/regulations_policies/policy_guidance/noise/history].
- Validation: Where possible, benchmark against measured data and published validations; flag where models are extrapolations for novel configurations [https://ascent.aero/project/noise-model-validation-for-aedt/; https://ntrs.nasa.gov/api/citations/20210015134/downloads/IN21_AAM_v12.pdf].

## Main Body

## S1. BagIt in brief: purpose, structure, guarantees, limits
The BagIt File Packaging Format defines a set of hierarchical file layout conventions to support storage and network transfer of arbitrary content. A “bag” is a directory comprising payload files plus “tag” files—metadata that document the bag and provide fixity manifests. Critically, BagIt’s design allows direct access to files using the native filesystem, unlike serialized archives (ZIP/TAR); at the same time it emphasizes strong integrity assurances using cryptographic-quality hashes. The RFC highlights two central advantages versus serialized archival formats: strong integrity assurances and direct file access [https://datatracker.ietf.org/doc/html/rfc8493].

Practical implications:
- Integrity and verification: Manifests record checksums for payload files, enabling verification after transfer or over time. The format supports cryptographic-quality algorithms and allows in-place upgrades to stronger algorithms without breaking backward compatibility—important for long-term sustainability of fixity practices [https://datatracker.ietf.org/doc/html/rfc8493].
- Direct access without unpacking: Because a bag is a live filesystem hierarchy, operators can inspect or copy specific files without re-materializing an entire archive [https://datatracker.ietf.org/doc/html/rfc8493].
- Scope: BagIt is agnostic about payload semantics and file formats; it focuses on structural, packaging-level integrity rather than object-level version history or content semantics [https://datatracker.ietf.org/doc/html/rfc8493].

Limitations to foreground (for later comparison):
- No native object versioning/history: BagIt verifies what is there, but it does not model object versions over time. The OCFL spec underscores this contrast when illustrating how a BagIt structure can be placed inside an OCFL object to add version control [https://ocfl.io/latest/spec/#example-bagit-in-ocfl].
- Minimal assumptions about metadata semantics: Tag files exist but do not prescribe comprehensive, preservation-specific metadata models; BagIt’s focus remains transfer and basic integrity [https://datatracker.ietf.org/doc/html/rfc8493].

## S2. OCFL in brief: scope, aims, and object model
The Oxford Common File Layout (OCFL) is an application-independent layout for storing digital information in a structured, transparent, and predictable manner, designed to promote long-term object management best practices. The official overview articulates five benefits that encapsulate OCFL’s design objectives: completeness, parsability, robustness (including against migration), versioning, and storage diversity (filesystems and cloud) [https://ocfl.io/].

Key characteristics drawn from the current specification:
- Completeness and self-descriptiveness: An OCFL object co-locates content and administrative information so that the object remains intelligible without the original repository software. The inventory provides the “map” of object contents and state [https://ocfl.io/latest/spec/]; [https://ocfl.io/].
- Human/machine parsability: The structure and inventory are designed to be read by both humans and software, enabling future access even with lightweight tooling [https://ocfl.io/].
- Versioning as a first-class concept: OCFL stores objects as sequences of versions (immutably preserving prior versions). Implementations can recreate past states from the recorded deltas/state, with a lineage that supports authenticity and audit [https://ocfl.io/latest/spec/]; [https://ocfl.io/].
- Robustness and migration-friendliness: OCFL builds in fixity and content-addressable mapping so that integrity can be checked across migrations and storage changes, reducing the risk of silent corruption or miscopying [https://ocfl.io/latest/spec/]; [https://ocfl.io/].
- Storage diversity: OCFL is designed for conventional filesystems and cloud object stores, to be resilient to infrastructure shifts [https://ocfl.io/].

The latest published version of the specification was updated on 7 November 2024, signaling active maintenance and maturation of the standard [https://ocfl.io/latest/spec/].

## S3. BagIt vs. OCFL: key differences
Framing difference: packaging for transfer vs. layout for at-rest preservation.

- Versioning and history
  - BagIt: No native versioning mechanism; a bag packages “a state,” not a lineage [https://ocfl.io/latest/spec/#example-bagit-in-ocfl].
  - OCFL: Models object versions explicitly, with immutable historical states and an inventory that can reconstruct prior versions [https://ocfl.io/latest/spec/]; [https://ocfl.io/].

- Integrity and fixity model
  - BagIt: Payload checksums recorded in manifests; supports cryptographic-quality algorithms and algorithm agility (adding stronger manifests without breaking compatibility) [https://datatracker.ietf.org/doc/html/rfc8493].
  - OCFL: Content-addressable storage and inventory-driven mapping of object state allows robust fixity verification over time and across migrations; integrity is tied to object structure and version state, not just a flat manifest [https://ocfl.io/latest/spec/]; [https://ocfl.io/].

- Parsability and intelligibility
  - BagIt: Simple, filesystem-native organization designed for transport and basic verification [https://datatracker.ietf.org/doc/html/rfc8493].
  - OCFL: Human- and machine-parseable inventory and predictable layout intended to ensure future intelligibility with minimal tooling [https://ocfl.io/].

- Separation of logical vs. physical organization
  - BagIt: Manifests directly enumerate payload files; logical vs physical mapping is not modeled as a versioned, object-level abstraction [https://datatracker.ietf.org/doc/html/rfc8493].
  - OCFL: Inventory separates logical paths (object’s intended file structure per version) from content addressing/physical paths, enabling renames/moves across versions without losing provenance of bitstreams [https://ocfl.io/latest/spec/].

- Migration and storage diversity
  - BagIt: Emphasizes reliable transfer and direct filesystem access; not designed around multi-backend persistence over decades [https://datatracker.ietf.org/doc/html/rfc8493].
  - OCFL: Explicitly targets robustness across storage technologies (including cloud object stores) with technology-agnostic verification [https://ocfl.io/]; [https://ocfl.io/latest/spec/].

- Conceptual purpose
  - BagIt: “Bag it and tag it” for trustworthy interchange/ingest; differs from serialized formats via integrity and direct access [https://datatracker.ietf.org/doc/html/rfc8493].
  - OCFL: Long-term at-rest object layout with preservation affordances (completeness, parsability, versioning, robustness, storage diversity) [https://ocfl.io/].

Community positioning: The OCFL initiative explicitly built on prior experience with BagIt (and Moab), aiming for interoperability and easy migration, rather than competition. The iPRES 2019 paper summarizes this lineage and intent [https://ipres2019.org/static/pdf/iPres2019_paper_45.pdf].

## S4. Interoperability: how BagIt and OCFL fit together
The OCFL specification includes a concrete example that demonstrates BagIt interoperability: “BagIt in an OCFL Object.” The spec notes that BagIt is a common packaging specification but does not provide a mechanism for content versioning; by using OCFL, it is possible to store a BagIt structure with versioning such that the logical state resolves to a valid BagIt bag [https://ocfl.io/latest/spec/#example-bagit-in-ocfl].

What this enables in practice:
- Versioned BagIt states: Repositories can maintain a series of BagIt “snapshots” as OCFL versions, preserving the full lineage while still being able to emit or reconstruct a standards-compliant BagIt bag for transfer or exchange when needed [https://ocfl.io/latest/spec/#example-bagit-in-ocfl].
- Pipeline complementarity: Ingest pipelines can continue to accept BagIt bags from creators or partners (leveraging BagIt’s broad ecosystem and simplicity), while an OCFL-compliant backend stores the canonical object with versioning, preserving history and enabling durable stewardship [https://datatracker.ietf.org/doc/html/rfc8493]; [https://ocfl.io/latest/spec/].
- Migration friendliness: OCFL’s inventory and fixity model allow repositories to verify integrity when moving or rehydrating BagIt content inside the OCFL object during system changes, reducing risk of loss or corruption [https://ocfl.io/latest/spec/].

Strategically, OCFL positions BagIt and OCFL as complementary approaches—BagIt for transport and interchange; OCFL for at-rest preservation with durable versioning and intelligibility. The iPRES 2019 paper emphasizes that OCFL set out to avoid pitfalls while supporting interoperability and easy migration from existing approaches like BagIt [https://ipres2019.org/static/pdf/iPres2019_paper_45.pdf].

## S5. Novel preservation strategies introduced (or consolidated) by OCFL
OCFL’s design concentrates several preservation strategies that go beyond (or formalize) what pure transfer packaging offers:

- Object completeness and self-descriptiveness
  - OCFL co-locates content with the administrative information needed to understand it, enabling reconstruction and interpretation without the original repository application. This improves survivability under software obsolescence and institutional change [https://ocfl.io/]; [https://ocfl.io/latest/spec/].

- Human/machine parsability
  - The layout and inventory are intentionally parsable by both humans and simple tools. This lowers the barrier to access and audit in the future, supporting the principle that preserved materials should remain renderable and understandable without bespoke systems [https://ocfl.io/]; [https://ocfl.io/latest/spec/].

- Versioning with immutable history
  - OCFL explicitly models object versions, recording state changes over time and allowing recreation of past states. Immutability of prior versions supports authenticity and provenance, aligning with the preservation goal of accurate rendering of authenticated content over time [https://ocfl.io/latest/spec/]; [https://ocfl.io/].

- Inventory that decouples logical and physical structure
  - By mapping logical paths (the object’s intended structure) to content digests/locations, OCFL allows renames and reorganizations across versions without losing the provenance of the underlying bitstreams. This separation is crucial when content is reorganized for operational reasons while maintaining intellectual continuity [https://ocfl.io/latest/spec/].

- Content-addressable fixity and migration verification
  - OCFL uses digests to bind content identity to inventory. This enables strong fixity checking during routine audits and, importantly, across migrations between storage systems. The result is technology-agnostic verification that the object “remains fixed,” even as infrastructure changes [https://ocfl.io/latest/spec/]; [https://ocfl.io/].

- Storage diversity and cloud readiness
  - OCFL is written with an eye toward diverse storage backends, including cloud object stores, to ensure the layout remains viable as institutions transition between on-premises and cloud environments [https://ocfl.io/].

Collectively, these strategies orient OCFL toward long-term durability: an OCFL object aims to be a complete, intelligible, self-verifying, versioned record of a digital object’s lifecycle, rather than a one-time transfer bundle.

## S6. Long-term stewardship challenges OCFL was designed to solve
Long-term digital preservation faces several recognized challenges: keeping content accessible and usable over time; ensuring integrity and authenticity; dealing with hardware/media and software obsolescence; maintaining provenance/audit trails; and avoiding lock-in via open, well-documented approaches [https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Digital_preservation]. OCFL’s design maps directly to these challenges:

- Integrity over time and across migrations
  - Challenge: Ensuring that digital objects do not silently change and that their exact bitstreams can be verified over decades and across storage transitions [https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Digital_preservation].
  - OCFL response: Content-addressable fixity and an inventory that catalogues object state enable robust audit and verification during regular checks and migrations [https://ocfl.io/latest/spec/]; [https://ocfl.io/].

- Authenticity, provenance, and version history
  - Challenge: Proving what changed when, by whom, and preserving earlier states to maintain trustworthiness of the record [https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Digital_preservation].
  - OCFL response: Built-in versioning with immutable prior versions allows repositories to reconstruct and attest to the full change history of an object [https://ocfl.io/latest/spec/]; [https://ocfl.io/].

- Software and institutional change (parsability and intelligibility)
  - Challenge: Original repository software may become unavailable or unsupported; objects must remain understandable without it [https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Digital_preservation].
  - OCFL response: Self-descriptive inventory and human/machine parsability allow understanding and re-implementation with minimal toolchains, protecting against software obsolescence [https://ocfl.io/].

- Storage technology shifts (on-prem to cloud and back)
  - Challenge: Infrastructure evolves; preservation systems must endure transitions without sacrificing integrity or usability [https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Digital_preservation].
  - OCFL response: Storage diversity as a first-order goal (including cloud object stores) and technology-agnostic verification support durability through change [https://ocfl.io/].

- Completeness and co-location of metadata and content
  - Challenge: Over time, metadata and content can become separated or unreadable in proprietary stores, hampering access and appraisal [https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Digital_preservation].
  - OCFL response: Encourages storing descriptive/administrative information with content so the object can be fully understood in isolation [https://ocfl.io/]; [https://ocfl.io/latest/spec/].

These design choices are not incidental. The OCFL community explicitly formulated five primary requirements—completeness, parsability, versioning, robustness, and storage diversity—as the backbone of the specification’s preservation posture [https://ocfl.io/]; see also the rationale in the community’s early articulation of goals [https://ipres2019.org/static/pdf/iPres2019_paper_45.pdf].

## S7. Implementation considerations and risks
- “Source of truth” positioning: OCFL as canonical vs. “backup-like”
  - Some practitioners frame OCFL as a “backup in a specific standard format” intended for long-term preservation, raising the question of whether OCFL should be the repository’s primary source of truth or a synchronized preservation corpus beside an operational database. Both approaches exist; the key is consistency and the ability to regenerate operational views from OCFL when needed [https://bibwild.wordpress.com/2023/03/21/ocfl-and-source-of-truth-two-options/]. Institutions should decide based on their operational constraints, but OCFL’s human/machine parsability and completeness make it capable of serving as the definitive record [https://ocfl.io/]; [https://ocfl.io/latest/spec/].

- Algorithm agility and fixity evolution
  - BagIt explicitly supports “in-place” upgrades to stronger hash algorithms without breaking compatibility, which is important for long-term integrity checking as cryptographic advice changes [https://datatracker.ietf.org/doc/html/rfc8493]. OCFL’s digest-driven model similarly anticipates algorithm updates within the inventory paradigm [https://ocfl.io/latest/spec/]. Repositories should plan for periodic fixity algorithm reviews and dual-manifest periods during transitions.

- Coexistence with BagIt in pipelines
  - A common pattern is to accept/exchange BagIt bags at boundaries (ingest, deposit, interchange) while maintaining canonical OCFL storage internally. OCFL’s “BagIt in an OCFL Object” example shows how to preserve transfer semantics while gaining versioned stewardship at rest [https://ocfl.io/latest/spec/#example-bagit-in-ocfl]; [https://datatracker.ietf.org/doc/html/rfc8493].

- Cloud and hybrid storage
  - OCFL’s storage diversity goal and technology-agnostic verification fit hybrid and cloud environments [https://ocfl.io/]. Teams should ensure cloud storage lifecycle policies and object immutability settings align with OCFL’s versioning/immutability expectations.

- Operational simplicity and human review
  - One of OCFL’s strengths is human/machine parsability for future-proof access [https://ocfl.io/]. Teams should preserve that advantage by avoiding opaque, externalized configurations that prevent an operator from understanding an OCFL object with basic tools.

## Recommendations
1) Adopt a dual-role strategy: use BagIt for transfer/interchange; use OCFL as the canonical at-rest layout.
- Rationale: BagIt excels at simple, verifiable packaging for ingestion and exchange; OCFL adds versioning, completeness, and migration-robust verification for long-term stewardship [https://datatracker.ietf.org/doc/html/rfc8493]; [https://ocfl.io/]; [https://ocfl.io/latest/spec/].

2) Where interchange semantics must be preserved, embed BagIt within OCFL.
- Action: Implement the OCFL “BagIt in an OCFL Object” pattern for objects that need to be re-exportable as standard bags. This captures a versioned lineage and still allows bag reconstruction [https://ocfl.io/latest/spec/#example-bagit-in-ocfl].

3) Institutionalize fixity algorithm agility.
- Action: Maintain manifests/hashes with current cryptographic guidance; plan for parallel digests during transitions (e.g., retaining older manifests until rehashing completes). BagIt and OCFL both support evolving fixity practices [https://datatracker.ietf.org/doc/html/rfc8493]; [https://ocfl.io/latest/spec/].

4) Treat the OCFL inventory as a preservation control plane.
- Action: Ensure the inventory remains authoritative for logical-to-physical mapping and version reconstruction. Validate regularly with automated audits and spot human reviews to sustain parsability and trust [https://ocfl.io/latest/spec/].

5) Engineer for storage diversity and migration.
- Action: Use OCFL’s technology-agnostic verification during storage migrations (on-prem to cloud, or between cloud providers). Test end-to-end integrity and reconstructability post-migration [https://ocfl.io/]; [https://ocfl.io/latest/spec/].

6) Decide deliberately on “source of truth” architecture.
- Action: Evaluate whether OCFL will be the canonical system of record or a synchronized preservation corpus. If the latter, ensure reliable, transparent sync and the ability to regenerate operational stores from OCFL when required [https://bibwild.wordpress.com/2023/03/21/ocfl-and-source-of-truth-two-options/].

## Conclusion
BagIt and OCFL occupy complementary positions in the digital preservation ecosystem. BagIt provides a simple, robust packaging convention with strong integrity assurances and direct file access that is ideal for transfer and interchange. OCFL, by contrast, is a preservation-oriented file layout for at-rest objects: it co-locates content and administrative information, offers human/machine parsability, and, crucially, models versioned history with technology-agnostic fixity and verification across storage environments. The OCFL specification explicitly demonstrates how BagIt can live inside an OCFL object, preserving interchange semantics while gaining versioning and durability.

OCFL’s novel strategies—versioning with immutable history, an inventory that decouples logical and physical organization, content-addressable fixity, completeness, and storage diversity—are calibrated to long-term stewardship challenges: authenticity, integrity through migrations, software and infrastructure obsolescence, and the need for self-descriptive, intelligible objects. The standard is current and actively maintained, reinforcing its suitability as a foundation for preservation architectures.

In practice, the most resilient approach is to use BagIt where it shines (ingest/exchange) and OCFL where preservation requires richer guarantees (at-rest storage and life-cycle management), leveraging OCFL’s BagIt example to bridge the two when needed.
